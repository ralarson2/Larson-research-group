Composting

Composting stabilizes organic materials through aerobic decomposition, producing a soil amendment that can support nutrient cycling and soil health.

What it is

Composting is an aerobic biological process where microorganisms break down organic materials in the presence of oxygen, generating heat, carbon dioxide, water vapor, and a more stable final product. The heat produced can reduce pathogens and weed seeds when piles reach and maintain adequate temperatures.

Why it matters

Composting can reduce volume and odor, improve handling and transport, and create a stabilized amendment that supports soil organic matter. Outcomes depend on feedstock mix, moisture and oxygen management, and how the compost is stored and applied.

How the system works

Composting relies on balancing moisture, oxygen, and the carbon-to-nitrogen ratio to support microbial activity. Materials are typically mixed (for example, manure with bedding or other high-carbon bulking agents) and managed as windrows, static piles, or aerated systems. Turning or forced aeration replenishes oxygen and helps regulate temperature.

As decomposition progresses, compost temperatures commonly rise into the thermophilic range, then decline as readily degradable material is consumed. Management focuses on maintaining aeration and moisture to avoid anaerobic zones that can increase odors and methane production.

Common composting approaches and outputs

On-farm composting approaches include turned windrows, aerated static piles, and in-vessel systems. Choice depends on scale, available space, labor, equipment, and weather protection.

Finished compost Stabilized organic matter and nutrients that can be used as a soil amendment. Nutrients are typically more slowly available than in fresh manure, and the material is often easier to store and apply.
Emissions Carbon is released primarily as carbon dioxide under aerobic conditions, but poor aeration or excess moisture can create anaerobic zones that increase methane. Nitrogen losses can occur as ammonia, especially when piles are warm and high in pH.

Benefits and tradeoffs

  • Can reduce odors and stabilize material, with potential pathogen and weed seed reduction when temperature targets are met.
  • Management is critical: moisture, aeration, and feedstock mixing strongly influence performance and emissions.
  • Ammonia losses can be meaningful, particularly for high-nitrogen feedstocks; covering piles, optimizing C:N, and minimizing over-turning can help.

Economics and the current market context

Economics depend on equipment needs (turner, loader, aeration infrastructure), labor, space, and the value of the end product. Compost can reduce hauling costs by concentrating nutrients and may create opportunities for bedding reuse or off-farm sales when markets exist.

Project evaluation should include feedstock preparation, seasonal constraints, leachate and runoff controls, and quality assurance for the intended end use.
