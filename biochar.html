Biochar

Biochar is produced through pyrolysis of feedstocks and is commonly used as a soil amendment, but designer biochars have the potential to be used in a variety of end uses.

What biochar is

Biochar is a carbon-rich material produced by pyrolysis, where biomass is heated at high temperature under oxygen-limited conditions. Pyrolysis generates three products: syngas and bio-oil (which can be used for energy) and the solid residual biochar.

Biochar is typically porous with high carbon content and low density, and it is often used as a soil amendment that can support soil health and nutrient management.

How biochar is produced

Slow pyrolysis is commonly used when the goal is to maximize biochar recovery. Operating conditions vary by reactor design, but slow pyrolysis is typically described across roughly 300–700°C with slow heating rates, and systems can be designed as batch or continuous operations.

Feedstock moisture matters for performance and energy requirements, and guidance commonly recommends keeping moisture below about 30% (or drying when higher) to reduce energy demand and reach target temperatures.

Why manure-based biochar is of interest

Converting separated manure solids to biochar can substantially reduce mass and volume, which can make storage and transportation easier compared to handling the original separated solids.

During pyrolysis, reported phosphorus recovery in manure-derived biochar is high, while nitrogen recovery is lower because some nitrogen is volatilized; recovery depends on temperature and operating conditions. However, when separated solids are used the majority of nitrogen is in organic form which improves nitrogen retention.

Biochar use

Biochar is used most commonly as a soil amendment, and it is also explored for filtration applications and integration into manure systems (for example, as a cover or as part of broader nutrient management strategies).

Research findings across studies show that biochar can improve nutrient retention and reduce nitrate leaching in some contexts, while crop yield responses can vary by soil, climate, feedstock, and application conditions.

Practical considerations

Costs and feasibility depend heavily on system scale, and whether energy recovery is integrated. On-farm deployment can also require pretreatment steps such as solid-liquid separation and drying to meet moisture requirements.
