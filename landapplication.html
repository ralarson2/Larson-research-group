Manure Land Application

Land application is the final step that determines how much manure nitrogen is retained for crops versus lost to air and water. Method, timing, and rate drive outcomes for productivity, water quality, and odor.

What it includes

Land application includes surface broadcast, incorporation or injection, and in-season application approaches such as irrigation or dragline systems. Equipment selection and field conditions determine placement, disturbance, and practical application windows.

Why it matters

The biggest controllable drivers of ammonia loss, odor, and runoff risk are application method and timing relative to weather, soil condition, and crop stage. Good application decisions increase fertilizer value and reduce risk.

Key decision points

Effective land application matches nutrient supply to crop demand while reducing the chance of loss. This typically means applying at agronomic rates, avoiding high-risk conditions (frozen or saturated soils, imminent precipitation), and using placement methods that reduce exposure to air and runoff pathways.

Rate and nutrient basis Rates are commonly set using nitrogen or phosphorus limits depending on nutrient planning and field history. Supplemental fertilizer nitrogen can be used when manure is applied to a phosphorus limit.
Timing Timing aims to avoid high runoff risk and align nitrogen availability with crop uptake. Spring and in-season strategies can improve nitrogen use efficiency compared to long lead-time applications when conditions allow.
Field conditions Soil moisture, compaction risk, slope, residue cover, and proximity to waterways influence runoff and infiltration. Setbacks and buffers remain important even with low-disturbance methods.

Common application methods

Application method influences ammonia loss, odor, and runoff risk primarily through how much manure is exposed on the soil surface and whether placement keeps nutrients in contact with soil.

Surface broadcast Fast and flexible, but higher odor and ammonia loss when manure remains on the surface. Rapid incorporation (where appropriate) can reduce losses and odor.
Injection or shallow placement Places manure below or into the soil surface to reduce ammonia and odor. Performance depends on soil condition and equipment; placement can increase disturbance in some systems and may be limited in very wet or rocky soils.
In-season application Includes irrigation or dragline approaches and systems such as “360 Rain” that aim to apply nutrients during crop growth. These approaches can better match crop demand but require careful management to avoid leaf burn, compaction, and losses during hot or windy conditions.

Benefits and tradeoffs

  • Injection and rapid incorporation typically reduce ammonia and odor compared to surface application, but may increase equipment cost and can be constrained by soil and weather conditions.
  • In-season application can improve nitrogen use efficiency, but demands tighter logistics and weather awareness and may require specialized equipment.
  • Runoff risk is driven by timing and soil condition; avoiding frozen or saturated soils and avoiding application before major precipitation events is often the most important risk reduction step.
