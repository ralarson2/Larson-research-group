Manure Management to Mitigate Climate Impacts

Manure management contributes to greenhouse gas emissions, primarily methane and nitrous oxide. Management choices across storage, treatment, and land application influence the magnitude and timing of these emissions.

Key emission pathways

Methane emissions occur primarily under anaerobic conditions during storage and treatment, while nitrous oxide emissions are associated with nitrogen transformations following land application. Carbon dioxide emissions are also generated through biological decomposition and energy use.

Why whole-system framing matters

A practice can reduce one emission source while increasing another if system interactions are ignored. Evaluating storage, treatment, and land application together helps avoid unintended tradeoffs and supports credible greenhouse gas accounting.

Management strategies

Practices such as anaerobic digestion, solid–liquid separation, composting, storage covers, and optimized application timing can reduce greenhouse gas emissions or shift them across the manure management system. The effectiveness of each strategy depends on system context and interactions with nutrient management.

Whole-system evaluation

Our research emphasizes whole-farm and life-cycle perspectives to avoid unintended tradeoffs. We evaluate climate impacts alongside nutrient losses, air quality, water quality, and economic performance to support climate-smart manure management decisions.
