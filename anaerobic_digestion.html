Anaerobic Digestion

Anaerobic digestion systems convert manure and other organic feedstocks into biogas and digestate.

What it is

Anaerobic digestion is a biological process where microorganisms break down organic material without oxygen, producing biogas and digestate. Biogas is mostly methane with carbon dioxide and small amounts of other gases, and digestate retains nutrients for land application.

Why it matters

Digesters can support energy independence, reduce odors and pathogens, and reduce greenhouse gas emissions by capturing methane that would otherwise be emitted from manure handling.

How the system works

Manure is collected and transferred to a sealed reactor where it typically remains for 5-30 days, depending on operating temperature. Most agricultural systems operate in the mesophilic range and have a retention period from 20-30 days, while thermophilic operation can shorten retention time but generally requires higher heat inputs and tighter management.

Digesters can process manure alone or integrate other organic materials such as spoiled feed, food waste, fats, or oils (known as co-digestions) to increase biogas production.

Common digester designs and outputs

Agricultural digester designs vary, but covered lagoons, plug-flow systems, and complete mix systems are most common.

Biogas Methane is typically the largest component of biogas (60-65%), and the gas can be used for heat, electricity generation, cooling, or upgraded to renewable natural gas.
Digestate Digestate is commonly separated into liquid and solids for flexible management. Digestion shifts nitrogen toward more plant-available inorganic forms, which can increase fertilizer value but also increase the risk of ammonia loss if not managed carefully.

Benefits and tradeoffs

Capturing and combusting methane lowers climate impact because methane is a potent greenhouse gas, and digestion can reduce emissions from manure handling and downstream storage because volatile compounds are degraded in the digester.

Digestion should be paired with downstream practices that minimize ammonia emissions, such as covered storage and injection or rapid incorporation during land application.

Co-benefits include odor reduction, pathogen inactivation, and antibiotic degradation under digester conditions.

Economics and the current market context

Economics depend on system scale, design, and the product pathway. Many existing agricultural projects historically generated electricity, while newer projects increasingly target renewable natural gas, influenced by policy-driven incentives and credit markets.

Reported capital costs vary widely by design and size, and ongoing operation and maintenance can be a meaningful fraction of total costs, so project evaluation needs to include realistic management requirements and downstream handling.
